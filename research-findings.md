# Research Findings: Palliative Care Volunteer Coordination Across Countries

## 1. Global Overview

Palliative care has developed rapidly and heterogeneously across the globe. In some countries, volunteers are the **only source** of care provision for patients at a palliative stage of disease, while in others, the use of unpaid workers is seen as exploitative. The cultural, legal, and economic context of each country profoundly shapes how volunteer coordination is structured.

**Key Reference:** *Organization of Palliative Care in Different Parts of the World* (Springer, 2024) — provides an overview of palliative care development across regions with case studies of delivery models.

---

## 2. iLIVE Volunteer Study (Multi-Country)

The **iLIVE Volunteer Study** is a landmark international project that supported the development and implementation of hospital end-of-life care volunteer services in **five hospitals across five countries**.

### Key Findings:
- Volunteer services providing direct support to palliative and end-of-life patients in hospitals are **new and developing**.
- Perceptions of volunteer services differ significantly between volunteers and healthcare professionals.
- Successful implementation requires structured coordination, clear role definitions, and ongoing support.

**Sources:**
- McGlinchey, T. et al. (2025). *iLIVE volunteer study: Volunteer and healthcare professional perceptions.* Palliative Medicine. [PMC12227806](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12227806/)
- EAPC Blog (2025). *Improving holistic palliative care support: perspectives from five countries.*

## 3. IAHPC Guidelines: Hospice/Palliative Care Volunteers

The **International Association for Hospice and Palliative Care (IAHPC)** provides guidelines emphasizing:

- Whether to use volunteers **must be a local decision**, based on local traditions, laws, and needs.
- In some countries, there is a long-standing tradition of weekly unpaid volunteering; in others, it is viewed as exploitation.
- Guidelines cover volunteer recruitment, training, supervision, and integration into care teams.

**Source:** [IAHPC – Hospice/Palliative Care Volunteers](https://iahpc.org/resources/publications/getting-started/hospice-palliative-care-volunteers/)

## 4. Volunteer Coordination Models by Region

### 4.1 Europe
- Well-established hospice volunteer traditions (UK, Netherlands, Germany).
- Formal training programs and certification requirements.
- Strong regulatory frameworks governing volunteer roles.
- The **ENDofLIFE project** (ERC-funded) studies how palliative care policies are adapted in diverse socio-cultural settings.

### 4.2 North America
- Hospice volunteering is deeply embedded in the US and Canada.
- Medicare-certified hospices in the US are **required** to use volunteers.
- Significant variation in volunteer coordination structures between urban and rural settings.

### 4.3 Asia-Pacific
- Rapidly developing palliative care infrastructure.
- Volunteer roles often influenced by religious and cultural traditions (e.g., Buddhist temple volunteers in Thailand).
- Limited formal coordination frameworks in many countries.

### 4.4 Africa
- Volunteers are often the **primary** source of palliative care provision.
- Community health worker models overlap with volunteer coordination.
- Severe resource constraints shape volunteer roles and expectations.

### 4.5 Latin America
- Growing palliative care movement with increasing volunteer involvement.
- Catholic Church and faith-based organizations play significant roles in volunteer coordination.

## 5. Staff–Volunteer Collaboration

A meta-ethnographic review (JPSM, 2023) found:

- Effective collaboration between volunteers and paid staff is **critical** for successful care.
- Common challenges include:
  - Failures in **coordination** and **information sharing**
  - **Role ambiguity** between staff and volunteers
  - **Tensions** within teams about scope of practice
- Successful models feature:
  - Clear role definitions and boundaries
  - Regular communication channels
  - Joint training sessions
  - Dedicated volunteer coordinators

**Source:** *A Meta-Ethnographic Review of Paid Staff and Volunteers Working Together in Palliative Care* (JPSM, 2023)

---

## 6. Barriers & Facilitators

### Barriers:
- Lack of formal training and support structures
- Cultural resistance to volunteer involvement in healthcare
- Legal and liability concerns
- Inadequate funding for volunteer coordination roles
- High volunteer turnover and burnout

### Facilitators:
- Dedicated volunteer coordinator positions
- Structured onboarding and ongoing training
- Recognition and appreciation programs
- Integration into multidisciplinary care teams
- Community engagement and awareness campaigns

**Source:** *Facilitators and barriers to volunteers' involvement in palliative care services* (JPSM, 2026)

---

## 7. Training Best Practices

Research highlights significant variation in training approaches:

- **Specialist palliative care settings**: Well-developed training programs exist.
- **Generalist settings** (residential care, hospital wards, community-based): Training requirements and best practices are less established.
- The **iLIVE project** developed international consensus recommendations for hospital end-of-life volunteer training.

**Source:** *Palliative care volunteering: Pressing challenges in research* (Palliative Medicine, 2022)

---

## 8. First Global Ranking of Palliative Care (2025)

The first-ever global ranking of palliative care (ScienceDirect, 2025) assessed countries using the WHO framework across 14 indicators. This ranking provides context for understanding the varying levels of volunteer infrastructure across nations.

**Source:** *First-Ever Global Ranking of Palliative Care: 2025* (Journal of Pain and Symptom Management)

---

## 9. Healthcare System-Wide Survey Findings

A cross-sectional survey across an entire healthcare system found:

- Volunteers are an important resource in **bridging** palliative care services and communities.
- No previous studies had systematically mapped volunteers' actual contributions.
- Support environments for volunteering vary widely and need optimization.

**Source:** *Volunteers in palliative care: A healthcare system-wide cross-sectional survey* (BMJ Supportive & Palliative Care)
